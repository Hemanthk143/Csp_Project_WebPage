History of cows

Domestic cows are one of the most common farm animals around the world, and the English language has several words to describe these animals at various ages. A baby cow is called a calf. A female calf is sometimes called a heifer calf and a male a bull calf. A heifer is a female that has not had any offspring. The term usually refers to immature females; after giving birth to her first calf, however, a heifer becomes a cow. An adult male is known as a bull. Many male cattle are castrated to reduce their aggressive tendencies and make them more tractable. Young neutered males, which are primarily raised for beef, are called steers or bullocks, whereas adult neutered males, which are usually used for draft purposes, are known as oxen. A group of cows, cattle, or kine (an archaic term for more than one cow) constitutes a herd. English lacks a gender-neutral singular form, and so “cow” is used for both female individuals and all domestic bovines.

Breeds of cows

Gir

  • Also known as: Desan, Gujarati, Kathiawari, Sorthi, and Surati
  • Origin: Gir forests of South Kathiawar in Gujarat
  • Skin colors: White with dark red or chocolate-brown patches
  • Horn shape: Peculiarly curved, giving a half moon appearance
  • Milk yield: 1200-1800 kgs
  • Age at first calving: 45-54 months
  • Inter calving period: 515-600 days

Red Sindhi

  • Also known as: Red Karachi and Sindhi
  • Found in: Karachi and Hyderabad district of Pakistan
  • Color: Red with shades varying from dark red to light
  • Milk yield: 1100-2600 kgs
  • Usage: Widely used in crossbreeding programmes
  • Age at first calving: 39-50 months
  • Inter calving period: 425-540 days

Diseases and Preventions

Cows can be affected by various diseases, but here are a few common ones and their preventions:

Bovine Respiratory Disease (BRD): Often occurs after stress such as transportation. Prevention includes vaccination, good ventilation, and avoiding overcrowding.
Foot and Mouth Disease: Highly contagious viral disease. Prevention involves strict biosecurity measures and vaccination in some regions.
Mastitis: Infection of the udder tissue. Prevented by maintaining hygiene during milking, proper nutrition, and regular health checks.
Viral Diarrhea (BVD): Caused by a virus affecting the immune system. Prevention includes vaccination and biosecurity protocols.

Preventions:

Respiratory Disease (BRD): Prevention includes vaccination programs, preconditioning, identifying and treating subclinically affected animals, and implementing biosecurity measures.
Foot and Mouth Disease: Control measures include vaccination, culling of infected animals, and strict biosecurity measures to prevent spread.
Mastitis: Preventive measures include maintaining hygiene during milking, proper cow nutrition, dry cow therapy, and regular health checks to detect early signs.
Bovine Viral Diarrhea (BVD): Prevention strategies involve vaccination, biosecurity protocols to prevent introduction of the virus, and testing and culling of persistently infected animals.
