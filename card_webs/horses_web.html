History of Horses

Horses have been integral to human civilization for millennia. Domesticated around 4000 BC, they have played vital roles in agriculture, transportation, warfare, and sport. Initially used for draft purposes, horses later became essential for rapid communication and trade across vast distances. Their relationship with humans evolved into companionship and leisure activities such as horse racing and equestrian sports.

Breeds of Horses

Thoroughbred

  • Origin: England
  • Height: 15-17 hands
  • Weight: 1000-1200 lbs
  • Color: Usually bay, chestnut, or gray
  • Characteristics: Fast runners, used for racing and sport

Arabian

  • Origin: Arabian Peninsula
  • Height: 14-15 hands
  • Weight: 800-1000 lbs
  • Color: Often chestnut, bay, gray, or black
  • Characteristics: Endurance and elegance

During our CSP project, we visited a local horse ranch where we met with the ranch manager. We were granted permission to survey the ranch and discuss various horse breeds and their care. The ranch houses multiple breeds, each with unique characteristics and uses. We shared insights on modern horse care technologies and nutrition to enhance their health and performance.

Habits and Food

Our research highlighted that horses thrive on a diet rich in hay, grains, and fresh vegetables. Hay provides essential fiber, while grains like oats and barley offer energy. Fresh vegetables such as carrots and apples provide vitamins and minerals. We advised the ranch manager on the importance of balanced nutrition and proper feeding schedules to maintain the horses' well-being and performance.

Diseases and Preventions

Horses are susceptible to various diseases. Here are a few common ones and their preventions:

Colic: A digestive disorder often caused by diet changes or stress. Prevention includes maintaining a consistent feeding routine and monitoring for signs of distress.
Equine Influenza: Highly contagious respiratory disease. Prevention involves vaccination and quarantining infected horses.
Strangles: Bacterial infection causing fever and abscesses in lymph nodes. Prevention includes isolation and vaccination.
Laminitis: Painful inflammation of the hoof laminae. Prevention involves managing diet and avoiding overfeeding.

Preventive measures:

Colic: Consistent feeding routine and monitoring.
Equine Influenza: Vaccination and quarantine.
Strangles: Isolation and vaccination.
Laminitis: Proper diet management and avoiding overfeeding.
